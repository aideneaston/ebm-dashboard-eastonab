Case Study Credibility
Case Study 1 – Intel Inclusive Leadership Program

Source Credibility
- Data from Intel's public diversity reports, an HBR case, and interviews with Intel's CDO
- Public company with external scrutiny and EEO-1 data; HBR adds third-party validation
- Reports detailed investment (~$300M), program phases, and outcome metrics

Context & Relevance
- Industry: Tech/semiconductor – not identical to all orgs but knowledge-work heavy and innovation-focused
- Size: ~110K employees – much larger than many organizations
- Time frame: 2015–2020 – recent, including intense DEI scrutiny years

Outcomes
- Improved representation in leadership, innovation metrics, and retention
- Shows that large-scale, behavior-focused training + accountability + system tweaks can move both diversity and performance.

Case Study 2 – Johnson & Johnson (J&J) Inclusive Leadership

Source Credibility
- Data from J&J D&I Impact Reports (2019–2023), interviews with CDO Wanda Bryant Hope, and an Organizational Dynamics journal case
- Public company, peer-reviewed case study → high reliability

Context & Relevance
- Industry: Healthcare/pharma, blending clinical and corporate knowledge work
- Size: ~135K employees; global operations
- Time frame: 2018–2023, including COVID-19 disruption

Outcomes
- Women in executive roles up (e.g., 29%→37%), retention and engagement gains, better patient satisfaction
- Uses mission-based framing (health equity) plus inclusive leadership training and equity-focused systems.

Evidence Quality & Consistency
Where the Sources Strongly Agree

Across practitioners and cases, there is clear convergence on:

- Training must be behavior-focused
    - Concrete skills (bias interruption, inclusive meetings, equitable evaluation), not just one-time awareness sessions.
    - One-off training has 80%+ failure rates.
- Accountability is non-negotiablele
    - Leaders and managers must have diversity/inclusion metrics in their performance reviews and bonuses (often 10–30%).
    - Metrics reviewed quarterly at senior levels.
- Systems must changege
    - Structured processes for hiring, performance, promotions, and pay ("bias interrupters") are necessary.
    - Not enough to rely on "good people doing the right thing."
- Timelines are realistic but not instantnt
    - 6–12 months to see behavior changes
    - 12–18 months to see team-level performance shifts
    - 3–5 years for full culture change
- CEO ownership mattersrs
    - CEO and top team must visibly own the agenda and review metrics regularly.
- Measure multiple outcomes
    - Don't just track demographic representation or training completion.
    - Also track engagement, turnover, promotion equity, innovation, and financials.

Where There Is Less Alignment or Uncertainty
- Sequence: Some emphasize fixing equity systems before/while hiring diverse talent (Washington), while Intel/Salesforce did both in parallel.
- Transparency: Agreement on internal transparency; external disclosure varies.
- Mandatory vs. voluntary: All big cases used mandatory training; some practitioners favor starting with voluntary pilots in more skeptical cultures.
- Scale & cost: Strong evidence exists for very large investments ($175M–$300M). The minimum viable level for smaller orgs is less clear.

Weaker evidence for:
- Small organizations (<500 employees)
- Highly resource-constrained settings
- Heavy blue-collar/union environments
- Non-U.S. regulatory and cultural contexts

Biases & Limitations
Key Bias Risks

- Selection Bias:
    - All three practitioners are successful diversity experts.
    - All three case studies are success stories from big-name companies.
- Survival Bias:
    - We hear from programs that worked, not those that failed and disappeared.
    - Practitioners do, however, note high failure rates of one-off training and show examples of "wasted" DEI spend.

- Confirmation Bias:
    - All voices are pro-DEI. There are no skeptics or alternative models (e.g., purely structural "no training" approaches).
    - Some risk that ambiguous results are interpreted positively.
